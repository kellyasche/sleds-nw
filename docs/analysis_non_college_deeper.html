Research Questions: Workforce Trajectories of Non-College Graduates in Northwest Minnesota

This phase of the analysis examines workforce participation trajectories among high school graduates in Northwest Minnesota who did not complete a postsecondary credential. Rather than relying on a single point-in-time workforce outcome, the analysis focuses on patterns of labor market attachment during the first five years after exiting formal education, with particular attention to persistence, instability, and regional attachment.

The research is guided by the following questions:

  1. Prevalence and Persistence of Workforce Disconnection

Among non-college graduates, how common is sustained workforce disconnection during the first five years after exiting formal education?

  • How frequently are individuals categorized as having not meaningful workforce participation or no Minnesota employment record during years 1 through 5 after exiting formal education?
  • To what extent does workforce disconnection reflect persistent patterns versus intermittent instability?

This question establishes the scope and depth of workforce disconnection in early adulthood.


  1. Workforce Trajectories of Disconnected Individuals

Among individuals classified as disconnected during the first five years after exiting formal education, what employment trajectories do they follow over time?

  • Are individuals persistently disconnected across multiple years, or do they move in and out of more stable workforce participation?
  • How do patterns of persistence and recovery differ across individuals?

This question shifts the focus from static outcomes to dynamic labor market pathways.


  1. Regional Attachment Among Individuals Without Meaningful Workforce Participation

For non-college graduates who experience workforce disconnection, where does employment occur when it is present?

  • Are individuals without meaningful workforce participation more likely to work within Northwest Minnesota or elsewhere in Minnesota?
  • Do patterns of regional attachment differ between persistently disconnected individuals and those with intermittent disconnection?

This question distinguishes regional underemployment from regional disengagement.


  1. Transitions Into Meaningful Workforce Participation

Among individuals who are initially disconnected but later achieve meaningful workforce participation, what characterizes those transitions?

  • At what point do individuals move into meaningful workforce participation during early adulthood?
  • Does the location of meaningful employment—within Northwest Minnesota or elsewhere in Minnesota—differ for individuals who transition later?

This question explores recovery pathways and the role of geography in supporting or constraining workforce stability.


  1. Stability of No Minnesota Employment Records

Among individuals categorized as having no Minnesota employment record during the first five years after high school, how persistent is this status?

  • Are individuals repeatedly absent from Minnesota employment records, or does this classification occur intermittently?
  • How often do individuals with no Minnesota employment record transition into other workforce participation categories?

This question helps distinguish long-term labor market detachment from temporary absence or mobility outside the state.


  1. Individual Characteristics and Workforce Disconnection

How are individual characteristics associated with workforce disconnection and subsequent employment trajectories?

  • How do factors such as economic disadvantage, English learner status, special education status, race and ethnicity, and Career and Technical Education (CTE) participation relate to persistent disconnection, intermittent instability, and recovery?
  • Which characteristics appear to support workforce stability and regional retention, and which are associated with disengagement or out-migration?

This question links observed workforce trajectories to potential leverage points for education and workforce policy.


Study Population and Group Definitions

Study Population

The analytic population consists of individuals who: - Graduated from a high school in Northwest Minnesota, - Did not complete a postsecondary credential (ps.grad = No), and - Have workforce participation records available for at least the first five years after high school graduation OR the first five years after exiting formal post-secondary education.


Definition of Workforce Disconnection

Workforce participation is measured annually during the first five years after high school or existing post-secondary attendance. Individuals are classified as disconnected in a given year if they are categorized as either: - Not meaningful workforce participation, or - No Minnesota employment record.

Using this annual classification, individuals are grouped based on the persistence of disconnection during years 1 through 5:

  • Disconnected (Broad Definition):
    Disconnected in three or more of the first five years after graduation.

  • Persistently Disconnected:
    Disconnected in four or five of the first five years after graduation.

  • Intermittently Disconnected:
    Disconnected in one or two of the first five years after graduation.

These definitions emphasize sustained labor market detachment rather than short-term transitions.


Regional Attachment

For years in which individuals are employed but not meaningfully attached to the workforce, employment location is classified as: - Within Northwest Minnesota, or - Outside Northwest Minnesota (elsewhere in Minnesota).

This distinction is used to assess whether workforce disconnection reflects regional underutilization or disengagement from the regional labor market.


Framing Consideration

Together, these definitions allow the analysis to move beyond point-in-time workforce outcomes and toward a trajectory-based understanding of early-career labor market experiences. This approach supports clearer interpretation of workforce instability, recovery, and regional retention among non-college graduates in Northwest Minnesota.


Methodology and Analytic Steps

This analysis examines workforce participation trajectories among non-college graduates in Northwest Minnesota, with a focus on identifying patterns of workforce disconnection, regional attachment, and employment stability during the first five years after high school.

Step 1: Data Preparation and Analytic Population

1.1 Analytic Population

The analytic population is restricted to individuals who: - Graduated from a high school in Northwest Minnesota, - Did not complete a postsecondary credential (ps.grad = No), and

  • Have complete workforce participation categorizations for years 1 through 5 after high school or,
  • first 5 years after exiting attendance in a post-secondary institution.

1.2 Annual Workforce Participation Classification

For each individual, annual workforce participation status is identified for: - grad.year.1 - grad.year.2 - grad.year.3 - grad.year.4 - grad.year.5

Each year is classified into one of the following categories: - Meaningful workforce participation - Not meaningful workforce participation - No Minnesota employment record.

1.3 Workforce Disconnection Indicators

For each annual observation, a binary disconnection indicator is created:

  • Disconnected (year-level) = Not meaningful workforce participation or No Minnesota employment record

Using these annual indicators, individuals are grouped based on persistence of disconnection across years 1–5:

  • Persistently Disconnected: Disconnected in 4 or 5 of the first 5 years
  • Disconnected (Broad): Disconnected in 3 of the first 5 years
  • Intermittently Disconnected: Disconnected in 1–2 of the first 5 years
  • Consistently Attached: Disconnected in 0 of the first 5 years

These categories emphasize sustained labor market attachment or detachment rather than point-in-time outcomes.

1.4 Regional Location of Not Meaningful Employment

For years in which individuals are categorized as not meaningfully attached to the workforce, employment location is further classified as: - Within Northwest Minnesota - Outside Northwest Minnesota (elsewhere in Minnesota) - Both (simultaneous employment inside and outside the region)

This classification allows identification of regional underemployment versus regional disengagement.

1.5 Employment Intensity and Job Holding

For each individual and each year (years 1–5), the following employment intensity measures are constructed: - Number of distinct employers during the year - Indicator for multiple job holding (two or more employers)

These measures are used to distinguish underemployment from precarious or fragmented employment.


Step 2: Descriptive and Summary Statistics

2.1 Distribution of Workforce Disconnection

Summary statistics are produced to describe:

  • The proportion of non-college graduates in each workforce disconnection category
  • Differences in persistence and intermittency of disconnection

This establishes the overall scope and severity of workforce disconnection in early adulthood.

2.2 Regional Attachment and Job Structure

Among individuals experiencing workforce disconnection, summary statistics examine:

  • The location of not meaningful employment (within region, outside region, both)
  • Average and median number of employers per year
  • Prevalence of multiple job holding

These summaries clarify whether disconnection reflects local underemployment, out-of-region work, or fragmented employment patterns.


Step 3: Relationship Between Individual Characteristics and Disconnection Trajectories

3.1 Variables of Interest

The analysis examines associations between workforce disconnection categories and key individual characteristics, including:

  • Demographics

    • RaceEthnicity

    • economic.status

    • SpecialEdStatus

    • Gender

  • High school characteristics

    • Dem_Desc
    • avg.unemp.rate
    • avg.wages.pct.state

  • High school academics

    • cte.achievement

    • total.cte.courses.taken

    • MCA.M

    • pseo.participant

  • Post-secondary

    • attended.ps

3.2 Analytic Approach

  • Categorical variables are examined using cross-tabulations and measures of association (e.g., Cramer’s V).
  • Continuous variables are examined using ANOVA.
  • Interpretation emphasizes substantive differences and patterns rather than statistical significance alone.

3.3 Stratified and Comparative Analyses

Where sample sizes permit, additional analyses compare: - Persistently disconnected versus intermittently disconnected individuals - Individuals disconnected within the region versus outside the region - Individuals who transition into meaningful workforce participation versus those who do not

These comparisons help identify which characteristics are associated with stability, recovery, and regional retention.


Framing and Interpretation

This methodology prioritizes a trajectory-based understanding of workforce participation, recognizing that early-career labor market experiences are dynamic rather than static. By combining persistence measures, regional employment location, and job-holding intensity, the analysis aims to provide actionable insights for workforce development and labor force planning in Northwest Minnesota.

The descriptive statistics paint a sobering picture of workforce attachment among Northwest Minnesota’s non-college graduates in the years immediately following high school. Taken together, three broad themes emerge.

Workforce disconnection is the norm, not the exception. Only 10.6% of non-college graduates maintain consistent workforce attachment across all five years after high school. The overwhelming majority — nearly 90% — experience some form of disconnection, with 58.2% persistently disconnected across four or five of their first five post-high school years. This rate is somewhat elevated compared to other Minnesota regions (which ranged from 40–55%), suggesting that Northwest Minnesota’s labor market structure, seasonal industries, and geographic position on the North Dakota border may compound the challenges already facing this population.

The “no MN employment record” category is the dominant and growing outcome. Across the annual snapshots, the share of non-completers with no Minnesota employment record grows steadily from 32.9% at year 1 to 40.7% by year 5, and accounts for an increasing share of the total disconnected population over time. While cross-border employment in North Dakota almost certainly accounts for a portion of this group — particularly in EDR 1 — the scale and persistence of this pattern suggests genuine labor market detachment is a meaningful driver as well. The county-level unemployment findings from the earlier analysis, which showed this group coming from lower unemployment areas, provide some support for the cross-border interpretation but do not fully explain the magnitude.

Marginal in-region employment is a transitional rather than stable state. The share of non-completers in not meaningful but in-region employment declines sharply over the five-year window — from 33.9% at year 1 to 21.5% at year 5. This suggests that marginal in-region employment is not a stable resting point but rather a transitional state from which individuals either graduate into meaningful regional employment or drift further from the Minnesota labor market. The steady growth of the “no MN employment record” category alongside the decline in marginal in-region employment suggests the latter transition is more common than the former.

Regional meaningful employment is stable for those who achieve it. Despite the overall picture of disconnection, the share achieving meaningful employment within the region holds relatively steady across the five-year window (20.8% to 25.7%), suggesting that regional workforce attachment — once established — tends to be durable. This is an encouraging signal for workforce development efforts focused on early career stabilization within the region.

The overall trajectory reflects a gradual sorting process. Over the first five years after high school, non-college graduates in Northwest Minnesota sort into two broad groups — a minority who stabilize into meaningful regional employment and a growing majority who drift further from Minnesota’s labor market. The window between year 1 and year 3 appears to be the critical period during which this sorting occurs, making early intervention particularly important for this population.

Summary of relationships

Across all variable groups, the persistence analysis reveals a consistent and sobering picture of workforce disconnection among Northwest Minnesota’s non-college graduates. The findings both reinforce and extend the point-in-time analysis, with a few important new insights emerging from the trajectory-based framework.

Strongest Predictors

CTE engagement is the most robust and consistent predictor across the entire analysis. Both CTE achievement and total CTE courses taken show clear dose-response relationships with disconnection persistence that hold across all comparison groups. CTE concentrators and completers have a persistent disconnection rate of 54.0% compared to 65.8% for students with no CTE involvement — an 11.8 percentage point spread that represents the largest practically meaningful difference in the analysis. Crucially, this relationship was also the most stable in the point-in-time analysis, suggesting CTE’s influence on workforce attachment is durable rather than transitional.

Attended post-secondary is the second strongest predictor (V = 0.080), with non-completers who had some post-secondary exposure showing meaningfully lower persistent disconnection rates (53.5% vs. 61.0%). This is a notable finding — even incomplete post-secondary attendance provides modest but lasting workforce attachment benefits.

Moderate Predictors

Dem_Desc is the strongest high school characteristics predictor (V = 0.098) and produces the single most striking finding in the analysis — entirely urban non-completers have a persistent disconnection rate of 77.5%, nearly 25 percentage points above the overall rate. This urban penalty is consistent across both the point-in-time and persistence frameworks and represents a clear geographic concentration of workforce disconnection risk.

Gender is the strongest demographic predictor (V = 0.086), with females persistently disconnected at a rate of 61.8% compared to 55.6% for males. This gap is consistent with the male-dominated industry composition of the NW region and holds across both analytic frameworks.

Weaker but Notable Findings

Race/ethnicity shows negligible overall association but notable distributional differences for smaller groups — Asian/PI, Black, and Hispanic non-completers have persistent disconnection rates 10–14 percentage points higher than White students, a pattern that warrants attention despite the small cell sizes.

Average unemployment rate continues to show a counterintuitive direction — persistently disconnected individuals come from lower unemployment counties — which is most likely an artifact of the North Dakota border effect and the “no MN emp record” ambiguity rather than a substantive finding.

Economic status, special education status, and PSEO participation all show negligible associations with disconnection persistence, suggesting these factors shape early transitions more than long-term workforce trajectories.

MCA Math scores show no meaningful linear relationship with disconnection persistence, reinforcing the earlier finding that academic proficiency predicts out-of-region mobility rather than workforce attachment among non-completers.

Key Themes for Policy and Practice

Three themes stand out for workforce development and education policy:

  • CTE is the most powerful lever available at the high school level. Its consistent, durable, and dose-responsive relationship with workforce attachment makes it the clearest policy target in this analysis. Expanding access to and depth of CTE participation among students at risk of non-completion should be a priority
  • The urban penalty demands targeted attention. Entirely urban non-completers face dramatically higher disconnection rates than their rural and mixed-community peers, suggesting that place-based workforce interventions may be warranted in urban communities within the NW region
  • Any post-secondary exposure helps. Even incomplete post-secondary attendance is associated with meaningfully lower persistent disconnection, suggesting that strategies to keep non-traditional students engaged in post-secondary — even briefly — may have lasting workforce benefits

High School Characteristics

Summary

Across the three high school characteristic variables examined, the findings are consistent with and reinforce patterns seen throughout the earlier analyses. Geographic context remains a meaningful but modest predictor of which type of disconnection non-college completers experience, while county-level economic variables continue to point primarily to the North Dakota border effect.

Key takeaways:

  • Dem_Desc is the strongest predictor in this group (V = 0.104–0.113) and produces the clearest geographic sorting pattern in the entire refined disconnection analysis. Entirely urban non-completers are dramatically overrepresented in the “no MN emp record” group across all five years, while entirely rural non-completers trend toward local underutilization (Group B). Town/rural mix students remain the most consistently attached group throughout. These patterns are stable across all five time points, suggesting they reflect structural features of the regional labor market rather than transitional effects
  • Average unemployment rate continues to produce the most counterintuitive and consistent finding in the analysis — the “no MN emp record” group comes from significantly lower unemployment counties than all other groups at every time point, with the gap growing over time. High school academics

Summary

Across the four high school accomplishment variables examined, CTE engagement emerges again as the dominant and most policy-relevant predictor of which type of workforce participation non-college completers experience, while academic proficiency adds a nuanced and important secondary signal. PSEO participation adds little independent information.

Key takeaways:

  • Total CTE courses taken is the strongest predictor in the entire accomplishments group, producing significant differences between nearly every pair of outcome categories at every time point. The gradient is clear and consistent — meaningful emp region takes the most CTE courses, followed by the marginal employment groups, with “no MN emp record” taking the fewest. This pattern holds across all five years without attenuation, making it the most robust finding in the refined disconnection analysis
  • CTE achievement tells the same story categorically — CTE concentrators and completers are most heavily represented in meaningful emp region and least represented in “no MN emp record” and “not meaningful - other.” The stability of Cramér’s V across all five time points (0.072–0.078) reinforces that CTE engagement is a durable differentiator of workforce attachment type, not just early career transitions
  • MCA Math scores produce the most nuanced finding in this group. By years 4 and 5, a clear hierarchy emerges — meaningful emp MN scores highest, followed by “no MN emp record” and meaningful emp region, with “not meaningful - in only” scoring lowest. This pattern has two important implications. First, it reinforces throughout the analysis that higher academic proficiency points toward out-of-region rather than regional employment. Second, the relatively high scores of the “no MN emp record” group compared to the marginal employment groups is the strongest academic evidence yet supporting the cross-border employment interpretation — individuals absent from MN records are not the least academically prepared group
  • PSEO participation is negligible and weakening across all five time points (V = 0.038–0.055), adding no meaningful signal for distinguishing disconnection type. Its earlier influence was entirely transitional

Overall, the accomplishments findings reinforce and sharpen the central themes of the analysis. CTE engagement is the most consistent and actionable predictor of both workforce attachment and the type of disconnection experienced. The MCA Math findings add an important interpretive layer — local underutilization (Group B) is associated with the weakest academic preparation, while the “no MN emp record” group (Group A) looks more academically similar to the meaningfully employed groups, further supporting the case that a meaningful portion of Group A represents cross-border employment rather than true labor force detachment.


pseo.participant

Cramér’s V is negligible across all five time points (0.038–0.055) and weakens over time. Key patterns:

  • PSEO participants are most overrepresented in “not meaningful - in only” (Group B) and “meaningful emp MN” in early years, but these differences largely dissipate by years 3-5
  • Meaningful emp region consistently has the lowest PSEO participation rate of any category (~15-16%), suggesting that PSEO — which predicts college-going and out-of-region mobility — is not associated with regional workforce attachment even among non-completers
  • The weakening association over time (0.055 at year 1 to 0.039 by year 5) is consistent with earlier findings that PSEO’s influence is transitional rather than durable
  • No consistent pattern distinguishes the three disconnection groups from each other on PSEO participation

Tracking note: Negligible and weakening predictor. PSEO adds little signal for distinguishing disconnection type.

Overall Summary — Refined Disconnection Category Analysis

The refined disconnection analysis adds important texture to the persistence findings by distinguishing between three types of workforce disconnection — no MN employment record (Group A), in-region marginal employment (Group B), and other disconnected employment (Group C) — and examining which individual characteristics are associated with each. Across all variable groups and all five time points, a coherent and interpretively rich picture emerges.

The Three Disconnection Groups Are Meaningfully Distinct

One of the most important findings of this analysis is that the three disconnection groups are not interchangeable. They differ systematically across multiple dimensions in ways that suggest different underlying mechanisms:

  • Group A (“No MN emp record”) is consistently associated with lower county unemployment rates, higher MCA math scores, more male composition, and more urban community backgrounds. This profile is more consistent with cross-border employment in North Dakota than with pure labor force detachment, particularly for individuals from EDR 1. However, the scale and persistence of this group — and its association with lower CTE engagement and lower post-secondary attendance — suggests genuine detachment is also a meaningful driver for a substantial portion of this group
  • Group B (“Not meaningful - in only”) is the clearest local underutilization group. It is associated with the highest share of never-attended post-secondary, the lowest MCA math scores of any disconnection group by years 4 and 5, more rural community backgrounds, more American Indian representation, and more special education students over time. This group appears to represent individuals who are present in the regional labor market but unable to achieve stable, meaningful attachment — a profile that points toward skill gaps, employer mismatch, or structural barriers to full-time employment within the region
  • Group C (“Not meaningful - other”) is the most heterogeneous group, combining out-of-region marginal workers, multi-employer workers, and unclassified employment patterns. It is associated with higher economic disadvantage, more Black and Hispanic representation, and lower CTE engagement than Group B. Its profile sits between Groups A and B on most dimensions, making it the hardest to interpret cleanly

Strongest Predictors Across the Refined Analysis

Total CTE courses taken is the strongest and most consistent predictor across all five time points and all three disconnection groups. The gradient from meaningful emp region (highest CTE) to “no MN emp record” (lowest CTE) is statistically significant at every time point and never attenuates. CTE achievement tells the same story categorically. Together, these two variables provide the clearest and most actionable signal in the entire analysis — greater CTE engagement is associated not just with lower disconnection but with a more favorable type of disconnection when it does occur.

Attended post-secondary is the second strongest predictor, with never-attended individuals most heavily concentrated in Group B (local underutilization) across all five years. Even incomplete post-secondary exposure is associated with meaningfully different disconnection patterns, with post-secondary attenders more likely to appear in meaningful employment categories or Group A than in Groups B or C.

Dem_Desc is the strongest geographic predictor, producing the clearest sorting pattern — entirely urban non-completers are dramatically overrepresented in Group A across all years, while entirely rural non-completers trend toward Group B. This geographic sorting is structural and stable, suggesting that place of origin shapes not just whether disconnection occurs but what form it takes.

The North Dakota Border Effect Is Confirmed and Sharpened

Across every relevant variable — county unemployment rate, MCA math scores, gender composition, and community type — the “no MN emp record” group consistently looks more like the meaningfully employed groups than like the other disconnection groups. This convergent evidence across multiple independent variables provides the strongest support yet for the interpretation that a meaningful portion of Group A represents cross-border employment rather than labor force detachment. This should be acknowledged prominently and consistently in any policy-facing write-up of these findings.

Key Themes for Policy and Practice

  • CTE engagement remains the most powerful and consistent lever for improving workforce outcomes among non-college completers. Its influence extends beyond whether disconnection occurs to shaping what type of disconnection is experienced, with higher CTE engagement associated with less severe and more regionally proximate disconnection patterns
  • Local underutilization (Group B) is the most actionable disconnection type for regional workforce development. These individuals are present in the regional labor market but not achieving meaningful attachment. They have the weakest academic preparation, the least post-secondary exposure, and the most rural backgrounds — a profile that points toward targeted skill development, employer engagement, and on-the-job training as potential intervention points
  • The “no MN emp record” group (Group A) requires careful interpretation before it can be treated as a policy target. Distinguishing cross-border workers from genuinely detached individuals within this group would require employment data from North Dakota and other border states, and would substantially change the estimated scale of true labor force detachment in Northwest Minnesota
  • Demographic characteristics alone are poor predictors of disconnection type, but equity-relevant patterns within the data — particularly for American Indian, Black, and Hispanic non-completers, and for female non-completers — warrant attention and should not be obscured by the negligible overall associations


Summary: Workforce Disconnection Among Non-College Completers in Northwest Minnesota

Overview

This analysis examined workforce disconnection among Northwest Minnesota’s non-college graduates using a trajectory-based framework spanning the first five years after high school. Rather than relying on a single point-in-time outcome, the analysis combined descriptive statistics on annual workforce participation, persistence of disconnection across years 1–5, and relationships between individual characteristics and both persistence categories and refined disconnection types. Together, these three components provide a multi-layered picture of workforce detachment among one of the region’s most vulnerable populations.

Scale and Persistence of Disconnection

Workforce disconnection among non-college completers in Northwest Minnesota is both widespread and predominantly persistent. Only 10.6% of non-college graduates maintain consistent workforce attachment across all five years after high school, while 58.2% are persistently disconnected — meaning they are absent from meaningful workforce participation in four or five of their first five post-high school years. An additional 11.8% are broadly disconnected (three of five years) and 19.4% are intermittently disconnected (one or two of five years). This rate of persistent disconnection is notably elevated compared to other Minnesota regions, which ranged from 40–55%, suggesting that Northwest Minnesota’s seasonal industry composition, the 1,000-hour single-employer threshold, and the North Dakota border effect compound the challenges already facing this population.

The annual snapshots reinforce this picture. The share of non-completers with no Minnesota employment record grows steadily from 32.9% at year 1 to 40.7% by year 5, while in-region marginal employment declines from 33.9% to 21.5% over the same period. This trajectory reflects a gradual sorting process — a modest share stabilizes into meaningful regional employment while a growing majority drifts further from the Minnesota labor market. The window between years 1 and 3 appears to be the critical period during which this sorting occurs, making early intervention particularly important.

What Predicts Disconnection Persistence

Across all variable groups examined, no single variable strongly predicts whether a non-college completer will be persistently disconnected. However, a clear hierarchy of predictors emerges.

CTE engagement is the most robust and policy-relevant predictor across the entire analysis. Both CTE achievement and total CTE courses taken show clear dose-response relationships with disconnection persistence that hold across all comparison groups. CTE concentrators and completers have a persistent disconnection rate of 54.0% compared to 65.8% for students with no CTE involvement — an 11.8 percentage point spread representing the largest practically meaningful difference in the persistence analysis. This relationship is durable, stable, and specific to regional workforce attachment rather than broader Minnesota employment.

Attended post-secondary is the second strongest predictor of persistence (V = 0.080), with non-completers who had some post-secondary exposure showing meaningfully lower persistent disconnection rates (53.5% vs. 61.0%). Even incomplete post-secondary attendance provides modest but lasting workforce attachment benefits — a notable finding given that PSEO participation alone showed no significant relationship with persistence.

Dem_Desc is the strongest high school characteristics predictor (V = 0.098), with entirely urban non-completers showing a persistent disconnection rate of 77.5% — nearly 25 percentage points above the overall rate and the single most striking finding in the persistence analysis. Town/rural mix students remain the most consistently attached group throughout.

Gender is the strongest demographic predictor (V = 0.086), with females persistently disconnected at a rate of 61.8% compared to 55.6% for males, consistent with the male-dominated industry composition of the NW region.

Economic status, special education status, race/ethnicity, MCA math scores, and PSEO participation all show negligible associations with disconnection persistence, suggesting these factors shape early transitions more than long-term workforce trajectories. The absence of a strong MCA math relationship is particularly notable — academic proficiency predicts out-of-region mobility rather than workforce attachment among non-completers.

What Predicts Disconnection Type

The refined disconnection analysis reveals that the three disconnection groups — no MN employment record (Group A), in-region marginal employment (Group B), and other disconnected employment (Group C) — are meaningfully distinct and are associated with different individual profiles.

Total CTE courses taken is the strongest predictor of disconnection type, producing significant differences between nearly every pair of outcome categories at every time point. Meaningful regional employment takes the most CTE courses (~5.4), followed by marginal employment groups, with “no MN emp record” taking the fewest (~4.2–4.3). This gradient is consistent across all five years and never attenuates.

Attended post-secondary produces the clearest sorting pattern across disconnection types — never-attended individuals are most heavily concentrated in Group B (local underutilization, ~68% never attended) across all five years, distinguishing this group as the outcome most associated with no educational exposure beyond high school.

Dem_Desc produces strong geographic sorting — entirely urban non-completers are dramatically overrepresented in Group A across all years, while entirely rural non-completers trend toward Group B. This geographic sorting is stable and structural.

MCA math scores add an important interpretive layer by years 4 and 5 — Group B (local underutilization) has the lowest math scores of any disconnection group, while Group A (“no MN emp record”) scores closer to the meaningfully employed groups. This convergent evidence supports the cross-border employment interpretation for at least a portion of Group A.

The North Dakota Border Effect

One of the most consistent and consequential findings across the entire analysis is the counterintuitive profile of the “no MN emp record” group. Across multiple independent variables — county unemployment rates (lowest of any group), MCA math scores (higher than marginal employment groups), gender composition (more male), and community type (more urban) — this group consistently looks more like the meaningfully employed population than like the other disconnection groups. This convergent evidence provides strong support for the interpretation that a meaningful portion of Group A represents cross-border employment in North Dakota rather than true labor force detachment. This caveat should be acknowledged prominently in any policy-facing interpretation of disconnection rates in Northwest Minnesota, as it suggests that reported disconnection rates are likely a ceiling estimate of true labor force detachment rather than a precise measure.

Key Themes for Policy and Practice

Three themes stand out across the full analysis:

  • CTE engagement is the most powerful and consistent lever available at the high school level. Its influence is durable, dose-responsive, and extends beyond whether disconnection occurs to shaping what type of disconnection is experienced. Expanding access to and depth of CTE participation — particularly for students at risk of non-completion — should be a priority for both education and workforce development policy in Northwest Minnesota

  • Local underutilization (Group B) is the most actionable disconnection type for regional intervention. These individuals are present in the regional labor market but unable to achieve stable, meaningful attachment. They have the weakest academic preparation, the least post-secondary exposure, the most rural backgrounds, and the highest concentration of special education students over time. This profile points toward targeted skill development, employer engagement, and on-the-job training as potential intervention points — particularly in rural communities where this pattern is most concentrated

  • The “no MN emp record” group requires careful interpretation before it can be treated as a uniform policy target. Distinguishing cross-border workers from genuinely detached individuals within this group would require employment data from North Dakota and other border states. Without this, the true scale of labor force detachment in Northwest Minnesota cannot be precisely estimated, and interventions targeting this group risk misallocating resources toward individuals who are already employed — just not in Minnesota
