Distribution of Employment Outcomes for Non-College Graduates

Among Northwest Minnesota’s 32,039 non-college completers, workforce participation patterns shift substantially in the years following high school graduation. In the first year after high school, the largest share of non-completers fall into the “not meaningful employment” category (32.7%), meaning they have some Minnesota employment but have not yet reached the 1,000-hour threshold with a single employer. A notable portion — about one in five (20.5%) — are still attending post-secondary in year one, a reminder that non-completion is often a delayed outcome rather than an immediate one. Regional meaningful employment accounts for 18.3% in year one, while just 4.1% have established meaningful employment outside the region but within Minnesota. Nearly a quarter (24.3%) have no Minnesota employment record and are not attending school, suggesting early detachment from both education and the regional labor market.

By year five, the post-secondary attendance rate drops sharply to just 4.5%, confirming that most of those who were still enrolled in year one have since exited. Regional meaningful employment holds relatively steady at 25.1%, suggesting the NW region retains a consistent core of its non-completing workforce over time. The share with no Minnesota employment record and not attending post-secondary grows notably — from 24.3% to 37.6% — which is perhaps the most concerning trend in the data. Meanwhile, the “not meaningful” employment category shrinks from 32.7% to 24.0%, indicating some individuals are either transitioning into meaningful employment or exiting the Minnesota labor market altogether.

At the 10-year mark, the “no MN employment record, not attending PS” category becomes the single largest group at 45.8%, a striking figure that suggests nearly half of non-completers have drifted away from the Minnesota labor market within a decade of finishing high school. Regional meaningful employment declines to 24.1%, and meaningful employment outside the region but within Minnesota sits at 10.6%. The “not meaningful” category falls further to 19.6%. The overall trajectory tells a concerning story: while a portion of non-completers do establish stable regional employment, a growing share over time appear to exit the Minnesota workforce entirely — whether through out-of-state employment, labor force non-participation, or other pathways not captured in the data. Given that Northwest Minnesota borders North Dakota, some cross-border employment likely accounts for a portion of this group, but the scale of the trend suggests that labor force detachment is a meaningful driver as well.

This analysis is going explore relationships and patterns of the portion of this population that found meaningful employment in the region vs. other employment outcomes.


Methodology

The above tables show the importance of understanding how non-college grads are navigating their post-high school pathways with a significant portion of them being disconnected from the regional workforce. To better understand these pathways, we will explore the relationships between independent variables (Demographic, High school characteristics, and High school accomplishments) along with workforce outcomes at 1-, 5- and 10-years after graduating high school.

Since we provided the descriptive statistics above, we now need to analyze which factors relate most strongly to outcomes, and then see if we can use those factors to build a predictive model.

For exploring the relationships we will do the following;

  • For categorical predictors (e.g., Race/Ethnicity, Economic status, CTE achievement, PSEO participation), use cross-tabs with Cramer’s V and chi-square.
  • For continuous predictors (e.g., MCA scores, total CTE courses, county-level variables), use ANOVA to compare means across the employment states.
  • Run these analyses separately for grad.year.1, grad.year.5, grad.year.10 to see how relationships shift over time.


Independent variables

We are going to continue using the variables that we have used throughout this project.

  • Demographic: RaceEthnicity, economic.status, SpecialEdStatus, Gender
  • High school characteristics: Dem_Desc, edr, avg.unemp.rate, avg.wages.pct.state
  • High school accomplishments: pseo.participant, cte.achievement, total.cte.courses.taken, MCA.M, MCA.R, MCA.S
  • Post-secondary: attended.ps


Relationships

We will first explore the relationships between the independent variables and the 1-, 5- and 10-year outcomes.

Demographics

Summary

Across the four demographic variables examined, none emerge as strong predictors of workforce outcomes for non-college completers in Northwest Minnesota. Cramér’s V values are consistently in the negligible-to-weak range across all time points, suggesting that demographic characteristics alone explain relatively little of the variation in where non-completers end up in the workforce.

Key takeaways:

  • Special education status is the strongest demographic predictor, but only at year 1 (V = 0.162), where it primarily reflects the much lower post-secondary attendance rates among special ed students and higher rates of early labor market disconnection. Its influence fades substantially by years 5 and 10
  • Economic status follows a similar pattern — most relevant at year 1 (V = 0.125) and diminishing over time — suggesting it shapes early transitions more than long-term workforce attachment
  • Gender is the most stable predictor across time (V = 0.072–0.080), with males consistently more likely to achieve regional meaningful employment and females more likely to land in not meaningful employment, likely reflecting the industry composition of the NW region
  • Race/ethnicity shows the weakest and most inconsistent associations (V = 0.048–0.056), though notable distributional differences exist for smaller racial/ethnic groups, particularly in the “no MN employment record” category by year 10

Overall, demographic variables appear to matter most in the immediate post-high school period and become less predictive over time, with the exception of gender which maintains a modest but consistent association throughout.


RaceEthnicity

Cramér’s V is negligible at all three time points (0.048–0.056), meaning race/ethnicity explains very little variation in workforce outcomes for this group despite being statistically significant. Key patterns worth noting:

  • White and American Indian non-completers have the highest rates of regional meaningful employment at years 5 and 10 (~25% each)
  • Asian/PI, Black, and Hispanic non-completers are substantially overrepresented in the “no MN employment record” category, particularly by year 10 (63%, 53%, and 58% respectively vs. 45% for White)
  • Small cell sizes for non-White groups warrant caution in interpretation

Tracking note: Weak predictor. Negligible Cramér’s V across all time points.

economic.status

The relationship between economic disadvantage and workforce outcomes is weak-to-negligible across all three time points, with Cramér’s V declining from 0.125 at year 1 to 0.084 at year 5 and 0.066 at year 10. While statistically significant at all points, the practical explanatory power is limited and fades over time.

Key patterns worth noting:

  • At year 1, economically disadvantaged students (status = 1) are notably less likely to be attending post-secondary (15.7% vs. 25.1%) and more likely to be in the “not meaningful” employment category (36.3% vs. 29.2%), suggesting earlier entry into the labor market
  • By year 5, disadvantaged students show slightly higher rates of “not meaningful” employment (27.1% vs. 21.1%) but otherwise similar distributions across outcomes
  • By year 10, differences largely converge, with the “no MN employment record” category being the dominant outcome for both groups (~44-48%)
  • The declining Cramér’s V over time suggests economic status is more relevant to early post-high school transitions than to long-term workforce attachment

Tracking note: Weak predictor, strongest at year 1 and fading over time.

High school characteristics

Summary

Across the four high school characteristic variables examined, none emerge as strong predictors of workforce outcomes for non-college completers, with Cramér’s V values and ANOVA effect sizes remaining in the negligible-to-weak range. However, some meaningful patterns emerge when looking at the nature of the relationships rather than just their magnitude.

Key takeaways:

  • Dem_Desc is the strongest variable in this group, with Cramér’s V strengthening over time (0.087 to 0.108) and a clear and consistent pattern — entirely urban non-completers are substantially more likely to have no MN employment record and less likely to achieve regional meaningful employment across all three time points
  • EDR is the weakest variable in this group (V = 0.055–0.063), adding little beyond what Dem_Desc already captures. The most notable EDR finding is the elevated “no MN employment record” rate in EDR 1 (Northwest), likely reflecting North Dakota cross-border employment
  • Average unemployment rate produces the most interesting and counterintuitive finding in this group — individuals with no MN employment record consistently come from the lowest unemployment counties, pointing strongly to cross-border employment rather than labor force detachment for at least a portion of this group
  • Average wages as a percent of state average tells a similar story to unemployment rate but with smaller effect sizes, further supporting the geographic border interpretation rather than adding independent signal

Overall, high school characteristics explain less variation in workforce outcomes than might be expected. The most actionable finding is the urban/rural distinction captured by Dem_Desc, and the geographic labor market context captured by the county-level economic variables, both of which point to place-based dynamics shaping long-term workforce trajectories for non-completers.

avg.unemp.rate

The county-level average unemployment rate shows a statistically significant relationship with workforce outcomes across all three time points, though the practical interpretation is more nuanced than a simple Cramér’s V allows. The Tukey post-hoc tests reveal a consistent and meaningful pattern: individuals with no MN employment record come from counties with notably lower unemployment rates than other groups.

Key patterns worth noting:

  • Across all three time points, the “no MN employment record” group consistently comes from counties with the lowest average unemployment rates, significantly lower than both meaningful employment groups and the “not meaningful” group
  • This counterintuitive finding likely reflects the North Dakota border effect — individuals from lower-unemployment northwestern counties may be crossing the border for work rather than being detached from the labor force
  • The gap between “no MN employment record” and “meaningful emp region” is the largest and most consistent difference across all time points, growing from -0.221 at year 1 to -0.417 at year 10
  • Meaningful employment groups (both regional and MN) do not differ significantly from each other at any time point, suggesting county unemployment rate does not strongly differentiate between types of employed outcomes
  • The pattern strengthens over time, suggesting that geographic labor market context becomes more relevant to long-term workforce trajectories

Tracking note: Moderate predictor with a distinctive and counterintuitive pattern. The “no MN employment record” group’s lower unemployment context is the most notable finding and warrants attention in interpretation.

High school accomplishments

Summary

Across the six high school accomplishment variables examined, this group shows the strongest and most consistent associations with workforce outcomes seen so far, though Cramér’s V values and effect sizes still fall in the weak-to-moderate range. Two distinct patterns emerge — one centered on regional workforce attachment and one centered on academic proficiency and out-of-region mobility.

Key takeaways:

  • CTE achievement and total CTE courses taken are the most policy-relevant predictors in this group, showing stable associations across all three time points and a clear dose-response relationship — greater CTE engagement consistently predicts higher rates of regional meaningful employment and lower rates of labor market disconnection. Importantly, CTE’s influence appears to be specifically on regional attachment rather than broader MN workforce participation
  • MCA Math, Reading, and Science tell a remarkably consistent story across all three subjects and all three time points — higher academic proficiency is associated with meaningful MN employment outside the region rather than regional attachment. This suggests that academically stronger non-completers are more likely to find employment elsewhere in Minnesota rather than anchoring to the NW region
  • PSEO participation is most relevant at year 1, where it predicts continued post-secondary attendance and lower rates of early labor market disconnection, but its influence largely dissipates by years 5 and 10
  • All three MCA scores are highly consistent with each other, suggesting a composite measure would be appropriate in modeling to reduce redundancy
  • The large amount of missing data for MCA Science is a limitation worth flagging in any write-up

Overall, high school accomplishments are the strongest predictor group examined so far, with CTE variables and MCA scores providing complementary but distinct signals — CTE pointing toward regional retention and academic proficiency pointing toward out-of-region mobility.


pseo.participant

The relationship between PSEO participation and workforce outcomes is statistically significant but weakens considerably over time, with Cramér’s V declining from 0.121 at year 1 to 0.050 at year 5 and 0.062 at year 10. The year 1 association is the most meaningful, driven primarily by differences in post-secondary attendance and labor market disconnection.

Key patterns worth noting:

  • At year 1, PSEO participants are much more likely to still be attending post-secondary (26.5% vs. 15.9%), which makes sense given that PSEO exposure increases the likelihood of continued college enrollment even among eventual non-completers

Overall Summary — Non-College Completer Workforce Participation Analysis

Across all four variable groups and three time points, a coherent picture emerges of what drives workforce participation outcomes for Northwest Minnesota’s non-college completers. No single variable dominates across all time points, but several consistent patterns and a clear hierarchy of predictors emerge.

Strongest Predictors

Attended post-secondary is the strongest statistical predictor at year 1 (V = 0.639) by a considerable margin, but its influence is largely mechanical — it captures whether someone is still enrolled — and dissipates almost entirely by year 10 (V = 0.032). It is most useful for understanding short-term transitions rather than long-term workforce trajectories.

CTE achievement and total CTE courses taken are the most practically important and policy-relevant predictors across the full time horizon. Unlike most other variables, CTE’s association with workforce outcomes is stable across all three time points and specifically tied to regional meaningful employment — more CTE engagement consistently predicts higher regional attachment and lower labor market disconnection. This makes CTE the strongest durable predictor of regional workforce outcomes in this analysis.

Moderate Predictors

MCA scores (Math, Reading, and Science) tell a consistent and notable story — higher academic proficiency among non-completers is associated with meaningful MN employment outside the region rather than regional attachment. This pattern strengthens over time and is highly consistent across all three subjects, suggesting a composite measure would be appropriate in modeling. The direction of this effect is particularly important — academically stronger non-completers appear to be more mobile rather than more regionally anchored.

Special education status is the strongest demographic predictor but only at year 1 (V = 0.162), where it captures early labor market disconnection and lower post-secondary attendance. Its influence fades substantially by years 5 and 10.

Dem_Desc is the strongest high school characteristics variable, with a stable and strengthening association over time (V = 0.087–0.108). Entirely urban non-completers are consistently the most disconnected from the MN labor market, while town/rural mix students show the strongest regional employment rates.

Weaker but Notable Predictors

Average county unemployment rate produces a counterintuitive but important finding — individuals with no MN employment record consistently come from the lowest unemployment counties, pointing strongly to cross-border North Dakota employment rather than pure labor force detachment for at least a portion of this group. This finding has meaningful implications for how the “no MN employment record” category should be interpreted throughout this analysis.

Gender shows a modest but stable association (V = 0.072–0.080) with males more likely to achieve regional meaningful employment across all time points, likely reflecting the male-dominated industries prevalent in the NW region.

Economic status and PSEO participation are most relevant at year 1 and fade considerably over time, suggesting they shape early transitions more than long-term workforce trajectories.

Race/ethnicity and EDR are the weakest predictors throughout, with negligible Cramér’s V values at all time points, though notable distributional differences exist for smaller racial and ethnic groups in the “no MN employment record” category.

Key Themes for Modeling

Three themes stand out for the next stage of analysis:

  • CTE engagement is the most consistent regional retention mechanism — it should be a central variable in any predictive model of regional workforce attachment
  • Academic proficiency predicts out-of-region mobility, not regional attachment — MCA scores point away from the region and a composite measure is appropriate to reduce redundancy
  • The “no MN employment record” category requires careful interpretation — county unemployment rate findings suggest cross-border employment is a meaningful driver for a portion of this group, particularly in EDR 1, which complicates any model that treats this category as uniform labor force detachment
