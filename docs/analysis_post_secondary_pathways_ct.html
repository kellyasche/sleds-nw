Dependent Variable

The cross-tab and ANOVA analysis exploring relationships between demographic, high school, and post-secondary variables with employment outcomes highlighted the importance of post-secondary variables in an individuals journey to meaningful employment inside and outside the region. Due to this, it makes sense to explore if some of the other variables play a role in post-secondary outcomes - whether an individual attends, graduates, the type of institution they attend and the location.

We will explore this here. We will explore this in three sections;

Tier 1: Core Postsecondary Completion

These outcomes are the most foundational and are most closely tied to long-term employment and economic stability.

  1. ps.grad (graduated from postsecondary)

Question: Who completes a postsecondary credential?
Why it matters: Credential completion is consistently the strongest predictor of sustained employment and earnings. This outcome captures whether students translate postsecondary participation into tangible labor market value.

  1. highest.cred.level

Question: What level of credential do graduates earn?
Why it matters: Certificates, associate degrees, and bachelor’s degrees lead to very different employment opportunities and wage trajectories. This outcome distinguishes not just completion, but the depth of educational attainment.

Tier 2: Secondary / Stratifying Postsecondary Outcomes

These outcomes add important context by differentiating how and where postsecondary education occurs.

  1. ps.grad.InstitutionSector

Question: From what type of institution do students graduate?
Why it matters: Institutional sector (e.g., public two-year, public four-year, private) shapes cost, access, program offerings, and alignment with regional labor markets.

  1. ps.grad.location (used when geography matters)

Question: Where do students complete their postsecondary education?
Why it matters: Location reveals patterns of mobility, regional retention, and place-based opportunity. It is especially relevant for understanding workforce pipelines and regional equity.

Tier 3: Early Access Outcome (Optional)

This outcome captures initial engagement with postsecondary education and helps contextualize later attainment.

  1. attended.ps (ever attended postsecondary)

Question: Who participates in postsecondary education at any point?
Why it matters: This provides a broad view of access beyond immediate enrollment, helping distinguish barriers to entry from barriers to completion.

Tier 1: Core Postsecondary Completion

Tier 1 Integrated Summary: Postsecondary Completion and Credential Level

Tier 1 examined two core outcomes: whether students complete postsecondary education (ps.grad) and the highest credential earned (highest.cred.level). Across both outcomes, clear and consistent patterns emerge.

Strongest Predictors Across Tier 1

PSEO Participation
PSEO participation is the single strongest predictor across both completion and credential level. Students who participate in PSEO are dramatically more likely to complete postsecondary education and far more likely to earn a bachelor’s degree. Early exposure to college coursework sharply differentiates both persistence and upward attainment.

Academic Readiness (ACT and MCA Scores)
Academic performance consistently predicts both whether students graduate and how far they go. Higher ACT scores and stronger MCA proficiency in Math, Reading, and Science are strongly associated with completion. These differences become even more pronounced at the bachelor’s level, where academic readiness clearly separates bachelor’s earners from associate earners and non-completers.

Economic Status
Socioeconomic disadvantage is strongly associated with lower completion and lower credential levels. Students eligible for free or reduced-price lunch are far less likely to complete postsecondary education and substantially less likely to earn bachelor’s degrees. Economic gaps widen at higher levels of attainment.

Special Education Status
Students who received special education services are significantly less likely to complete postsecondary education and are very unlikely to earn bachelor’s degrees. The disparity intensifies when examining credential level.

Persistent but Moderately Sized Predictors

Race/Ethnicity
Racial and ethnic disparities are substantial across both outcomes. American Indian, Black, and Hispanic students are far more likely to not graduate, while White and Asian/PI students are more likely to earn bachelor’s degrees. Inequities are particularly visible at higher levels of attainment.

Gender
Women are more likely than men to complete postsecondary education and to earn bachelor’s degrees. The gender gap is most pronounced at the bachelor’s level rather than at lower credential tiers.

CTE Measures
CTE participation and course-taking patterns are associated with different credential pathways. Higher CTE course volume aligns more closely with associate-level attainment, while bachelor’s attainment is associated with fewer CTE courses. These relationships are meaningful but not as strong as academic readiness or PSEO participation.

Weak Contextual Predictors

Geographic and Economic Context (EDR, Rural-ness, County Unemployment, County Wages)
High school contextual characteristics are consistently weak predictors across both completion and credential level. While regional differences are statistically detectable, geographic and local economic conditions explain only a small portion of variation in attainment.

Overall Tier 1 Pattern

Across both completion and credential level, the dominant drivers of postsecondary success in Northwest Minnesota are academic preparation and early college exposure, followed closely by structural demographic inequities. Geographic and local economic context play a comparatively minor role.

Importantly, disparities intensify at higher levels of attainment. Predictors that influence completion become even more pronounced when distinguishing bachelor’s degree earners from all other groups. Tier 1 therefore suggests that improving academic readiness and expanding meaningful early college opportunities are central levers for increasing both postsecondary completion and higher-level credential attainment in the region.

Demographics

1. Race/Ethnicity (Cramér’s V = 0.298)
Race and ethnicity show the strongest relationship with postsecondary graduation. Completion rates vary widely across groups, with White students having the highest likelihood of graduating and substantially lower completion among American Indian, Black, and Hispanic students. American Indian students are particularly overrepresented among non-completers, indicating pronounced racial and ethnic inequities in postsecondary attainment.

2. Economic Status (φ = 0.259)
Economic disadvantage is strongly associated with lower postsecondary graduation rates. Students eligible for free or reduced-price lunch are far less likely to complete a credential and are disproportionately represented among non-completers, highlighting persistent socioeconomic barriers to postsecondary success.

3. Special Education Status (φ = 0.254)
Special education status is also strongly related to postsecondary graduation. Students who required special education services complete postsecondary credentials at much lower rates than their peers, suggesting substantial structural and support-related challenges in translating secondary education into postsecondary attainment.

4. Gender (φ = 0.122)
Gender has the weakest relationship among the demographic variables examined, though it remains meaningful. Women are more likely than men to complete a postsecondary credential, while men are disproportionately represented among non-completers. Compared to other demographic factors, however, the gender gap is more modest.

High School Characteristics

1. Economic Development Region (Cramér’s V = 0.086)
Economic Development Region shows the strongest relationship among the high school characteristics, though the effect remains small. Students from EDR 4 – West Central have the highest completion rate (47.2%), followed by EDR 1 – Northwest (43.0%), while EDR 2 – Headwaters has the lowest rate (36.0%). Regional context appears to matter, but only modestly.

2. Rural-ness of School District (Dem_Desc) (Cramér’s V = 0.030)
District rural classification is statistically related to postsecondary completion, but the effect is very small. Completion rates vary only slightly across district types, ranging from about 41% to 47%. Rural-ness alone does little to meaningfully explain differences in postsecondary graduation.

3. Average County Unemployment Rate (ANOVA, p < .001)
Average unemployment during high school years differs statistically between completers and non-completers, but the mean difference is very small (≈ 0.13 percentage points). Local unemployment conditions during high school have minimal practical association with postsecondary completion.

4. Average County Wages as Percent of State (ANOVA, p < .001)
Average county wage levels during high school years are statistically associated with completion, but the difference is extremely small (≈ 0.01). County-level wage context provides very limited explanatory value for postsecondary graduation outcomes.

Overall Pattern:
Compared to demographic factors, high school contextual characteristics are far weaker predictors of postsecondary completion. While region shows a modest relationship, broader geographic and economic context variables explain only a small portion of variation in who completes a credential in Northwest Minnesota.

High School Accomplishments

1. PSEO Participation (φ = 0.299)
Participation in postsecondary educational opportunities during high school shows the strongest relationship with postsecondary completion. Students who participated in PSEO graduate at dramatically higher rates (63.1%) than non-participants (31.7%). Early exposure to college-level coursework appears to be one of the most powerful predictors of eventual credential completion.

2. ACT Composite Score (ANOVA, p < .001; mean difference ≈ 2.78 points)
ACT performance is strongly associated with postsecondary graduation. Students who completed a credential scored nearly three points higher on average than non-completers, indicating that academic preparation and college readiness play a major role in completion outcomes.

3. MCA Reading (ANOVA, p < .001; mean difference ≈ 0.51)
Reading proficiency shows a substantial relationship with completion. Students who graduated from postsecondary programs scored meaningfully higher in reading than non-completers, underscoring the importance of literacy skills for long-term academic persistence.

4. MCA Math (ANOVA, p < .001; mean difference ≈ 0.49)

Race/Ethnicity

Strong and Consistent Predictors

PSEO Participation (Cramér’s V = 0.332)
PSEO participation is the strongest predictor of institutional sector. Students who participated in PSEO are dramatically more likely to graduate from 4-year public institutions and far less likely to not complete postsecondary education. Early college exposure sharply differentiates bachelor’s-oriented pathways from 2-year and non-completion outcomes.

Academic Readiness (ACT and MCA Scores)
ACT Composite scores and MCA proficiency in Math, Reading, and Science strongly differentiate sector pathways. Students graduating from 4-year public institutions consistently outperform 2-year public graduates and non-completers. Academic preparation clearly separates 4-year institutional trajectories from other outcomes.

Economic Status (Cramér’s V = 0.271)
Socioeconomic status strongly shapes institutional sector. Students not eligible for free or reduced-price lunch are far more likely to graduate from 4-year public institutions, while economically disadvantaged students are disproportionately represented among non-completers. Disparities are especially visible in access to 4-year public pathways.

Special Education Status (Cramér’s V = 0.267)
Students who received special education services are dramatically less likely to graduate from 4-year institutions and overwhelmingly more likely to not complete postsecondary education. Sector-level disparities mirror and intensify completion gaps.

Moderate Predictors

Race/Ethnicity (Cramér’s V = 0.176)
Racial and ethnic disparities extend into sector pathways. White and Asian/PI students are more likely to graduate from 4-year public institutions, while American Indian, Black, and Hispanic students are more likely to not graduate. Sector differences reflect broader inequities in postsecondary attainment.

Gender (Cramér’s V = 0.149)
Women are more likely than men to graduate from 4-year public institutions and less likely to not complete postsecondary education. Differences are less pronounced in 2-year public pathways.

CTE Measures (Cramér’s V = 0.119; ANOVA p < .001)
CTE achievement and course volume are associated with sector alignment. Students with higher CTE participation are more likely to graduate from 2-year public institutions, while 4-year public graduates take fewer CTE courses on average. CTE pathways appear aligned with 2-year institutional trajectories.

Weak Contextual Predictors

Economic Development Region (Cramér’s V = 0.072)
Regional differences in sector outcomes are statistically detectable but modest. Region explains only a small share of variation in institutional pathways.

Rural-ness of District (Cramér’s V = 0.037)
District rural classification has a very small relationship with institutional sector.

County Unemployment and Wage Context (ANOVA, p < .001)
Local economic conditions during high school are statistically related to institutional sector, but differences are extremely small and offer little practical explanatory value.

Overall Pattern

Postsecondary institution sector in Northwest Minnesota is shaped primarily by academic readiness, early college exposure, and structural demographic inequities. Academic performance and PSEO participation sharply differentiate 4-year public pathways from 2-year and non-completion outcomes. Socioeconomic and special education disparities remain substantial, particularly in access to and completion of 4-year institutions. In contrast, high school contextual characteristics play only a minor role in determining institutional sector outcomes.

County unemployment during high school years differs modestly across graduation locations. Overall Pattern:
High school contextual characteristics are weak predictors of postsecondary graduation location. While regional differences are statistically detectable, rural classification and county-level economic conditions explain only a small share of variation in geographic graduation outcomes. Compared to demographic and academic accomplishment variables, high school characteristics play a limited role in determining where students ultimately graduate.

3. MCA Proficiency (Math, Reading, Science) (ANOVA, p < .001)
MCA scores in Math, Reading, and Science show strong relationships with graduation location. 5. CTE Achievement Status (Cramér’s V = 0.077)

Overall Pattern:
High school accomplishments—especially academic readiness and PSEO participation—are the strongest predictors of postsecondary graduation location. Students with higher academic performance are not only more likely to complete postsecondary education, but also more likely to graduate outside the region. CTE measures show moderate alignment with regional retention patterns, but their influence is notably weaker than academic indicators. Compared to high school contextual characteristics, academic preparation plays a far more decisive role in shaping geographic mobility of postsecondary attainment.

Summary

Tier 2 (Location) examines where students graduate—within the region, elsewhere in Minnesota, outside Minnesota, or not at all. Across predictor groups, clear themes emerge.

Strong and Consistent Predictors

PSEO Participation (Cramér’s V = 0.304)
PSEO participation is one of the strongest predictors of graduation location. Participants are far more likely to graduate in any location and far less likely to not graduate at all. Early college exposure strongly differentiates both completion and geographic mobility.

Academic Readiness (ACT and MCA Scores)
ACT Composite scores and MCA proficiency in Math, Reading, and Science strongly differentiate graduation locations. Students graduating outside the region or elsewhere in Minnesota consistently score higher than regional graduates, while non-completers score substantially lower than all graduate groups. Academic preparation is tightly linked not only to completion, but to geographic mobility.

Economic Status (Cramér’s V = 0.266)
Socioeconomic status strongly shapes graduation location. Students not eligible for free or reduced-price lunch are far more likely to graduate within or beyond the region and far less likely to not graduate at all. Socioeconomic disparities are evident in both attainment and mobility.

Special Education Status (Cramér’s V = 0.255)
Students who received special education services are overwhelmingly more likely to not graduate and far less likely to complete postsecondary education in any location. Disparities extend beyond completion into reduced geographic mobility.

Moderate Predictors

Race/Ethnicity (Cramér’s V = 0.173)
Race and ethnicity meaningfully shape graduation location. White students are more likely to graduate within the region or elsewhere in Minnesota, while American Indian, Black, and Hispanic students are more likely to not graduate. Asian/PI students show comparatively higher rates of graduation outside Minnesota. Disparities influence both attainment and geographic movement.

Gender (Cramér’s V = 0.125)
Women are more likely than men to graduate within the region and elsewhere in Minnesota and are less likely to not graduate. Differences in out-of-state graduation are smaller. Gender gaps are present but less pronounced than socioeconomic or special education disparities.

CTE Course Volume (ANOVA, p < .001)
CTE intensity shows a moderate relationship with graduation location. Students graduating outside the region tend to take fewer CTE courses, while non-completers take more. CTE pathways appear somewhat aligned with regional retention patterns.

Weak Contextual Predictors

Economic Development Region (Cramér’s V = 0.076)
Regional differences in graduation location are statistically detectable but modest. EDR 2 – Headwaters shows higher non-completion and lower regional graduation, but overall explanatory power is limited.

Rural-ness of District (Cramér’s V = 0.053)
District rural classification has a small relationship with graduation location.

County Unemployment and Wage Context (ANOVA, p < .001)
Local economic conditions during high school are statistically associated with graduation location, but differences are extremely small and offer little practical explanation for mobility patterns.

Overall Pattern

Postsecondary graduation location in Northwest Minnesota is shaped primarily by academic readiness, early college exposure, and structural demographic inequities. Students with stronger academic preparation are more likely to complete postsecondary education and more likely to graduate outside the region. Socioeconomic and special education disparities significantly constrain both attainment and mobility. In contrast, high school contextual characteristics contribute little to explaining where students ultimately graduate.


Overall Synthesis: Postsecondary Pathways in Northwest Minnesota

1. Cross-Tier Synthesis (Completion, Credential Level, Sector, and Location)

Across Tier 1 and Tier 2 outcomes, a clear structure emerges in how postsecondary pathways unfold in Northwest Minnesota. Whether examining completion (ps.grad), credential level (highest.cred.level), institutional sector (ps.grad.InstitutionSector), or graduation location (ps.grad.location), the same drivers consistently shape outcomes.

Students with stronger academic preparation and early exposure to college are far more likely to complete postsecondary education, earn bachelor’s degrees, graduate from 4-year public institutions, and do so outside the region. Conversely, students facing socioeconomic disadvantage, special education barriers, or weaker academic readiness are disproportionately concentrated among non-completers and 2-year institutional pathways, with more limited geographic mobility.

Importantly, stratification intensifies as outcomes become more selective. Gaps widen when moving from completion to bachelor’s attainment and from 2-year to 4-year institutional pathways. Tier 2 outcomes reinforce and amplify patterns observed in Tier 1.

2. Ranked Predictors Across All Outcomes

Strongest and Most Consistent Predictors Across All Outcomes - PSEO participation
- ACT Composite score and MCA proficiency

These variables consistently and powerfully differentiate completion, credential level, institutional tier, and geographic mobility.

Strong and Structural Predictors - Economic status
- Special education status
- Race/Ethnicity

These predictors reflect persistent structural inequities that influence not only whether students complete postsecondary education, but also how far they go and where they graduate.

Moderate / Pathway-Aligned Predictors - CTE achievement
- Total CTE courses taken

CTE participation aligns more strongly with 2-year public pathways and regional retention patterns than with bachelor’s attainment or out-of-region mobility.

Weak Contextual Predictors - Economic Development Region (EDR)
- Rural-ness of school district
- County unemployment
- County wage levels

High school contextual and local economic characteristics are statistically detectable but explain comparatively little variation in postsecondary outcomes.

3. Core Findings

Three overarching conclusions emerge from the full analysis:

  1. Academic readiness is the dominant sorting mechanism.
    Students with stronger academic preparation consistently access higher-tier institutions, earn higher credentials, and demonstrate greater geographic mobility.

  2. Early college exposure is the most powerful policy lever.
    PSEO participation dramatically shifts outcomes upward across every postsecondary measure examined.

  3. Structural inequities shape both attainment and mobility.
    Socioeconomic disadvantage, special education status, and racial disparities meaningfully constrain both completion and access to higher-tier and more mobile postsecondary pathways.

Postsecondary outcomes in the region are not random—they are patterned, predictable, and strongly structured by readiness and access.

4. Implications for Policy and Regional Strategy

The findings suggest several strategic considerations:

  • Expanding access to rigorous academic preparation and strengthening readiness pipelines may have the largest long-term impact on both completion and bachelor’s production.
  • Increasing equitable access to PSEO could serve as a high-leverage intervention for shifting students into higher-tier and more mobile pathways.
  • Addressing socioeconomic and special education disparities must remain central to any strategy aimed at increasing regional attainment.
  • CTE pathways play an important role in supporting 2-year and locally aligned workforce outcomes, but they are not substitutes for academic preparation when the goal is bachelor’s attainment.
  • Geographic context alone does not explain postsecondary outcomes; student-level preparation and structural inequities matter far more than rural classification or local labor market conditions.

Taken together, this analysis indicates that improving academic readiness and expanding equitable access to early college opportunities are the most direct levers for strengthening postsecondary success and long-term workforce outcomes in Northwest Minnesota.

Most Important Variables Related to Graduating Within the Region

The variables most strongly and consistently associated with graduating within the region were:

PSEO Participation
Early college exposure dramatically increased the likelihood of postsecondary completion, which in turn increased the likelihood of graduating within the region. PSEO is one of the most powerful levers influencing regional graduation outcomes.

Academic Readiness (ACT and MCA Scores)

Economic Status
Socioeconomic disadvantage substantially reduced the likelihood of regional graduation, largely through its strong relationship with non-completion.

Special Education Status
Students who received special education services were far less likely to graduate in any location, including within the region. This was one of the strongest structural constraints observed.

Most Important Variables Related to 2-Year / Associate-Level Pathways

The variables most strongly associated with graduating from a 2-year institution and/or earning an associate degree or lesser credential were:

Academic Readiness (ACT and MCA Scores)
Academic preparation strongly sorted students into institutional tiers. Students in 2-year pathways had significantly lower academic performance than those in 4-year public institutions, though higher than non-completers.

PSEO Participation
Students who did not participate in PSEO were much more likely to follow 2-year pathways or not complete. PSEO participation strongly shifted students toward bachelor’s attainment.

CTE Participation and Course Volume
Higher CTE course-taking aligned closely with 2-year public institutional pathways and associate-level attainment. CTE intensity was one of the clearest pathway-alignment variables.

Economic Status and Special Education Status
Structural inequities were also evident in 2-year pathways. Economically disadvantaged students and students receiving special education services were more concentrated in associate-level or non-completion outcomes than in bachelor’s pathways.
